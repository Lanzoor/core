Yes, this server is definitely not learning-focused. The server is misleading to members who join for learning.

A vast majority of people in this server are rather active in #general-social instead, and are mostly here to casually talk with others rather than study or exchange knowledge about the language itself. 🚪 Newcomers are NOT welcome here. 🫷

This section applies to newcomers.
There are several issues that newcomers tend to encounter when trying to engage with the community. By engaging in this server, you acknowledge these issues and accept the experience that comes with them.

  • About the Matthew Effect

    Have you ever heard of the Matthew Effect? The Matthew Effect states that those who are already well-known continue to receive more attention 📈, while newcomers often struggle to be noticed. 📉 Over time, this creates an environment where the same familiar individuals dominate conversations, while new members find it increasingly difficult to enter ongoing discussions or gain visibility.

    I believe this server is an exceptionally strong example of the Matthew Effect in action. Once someone becomes a recognizable or active member of the community, their messages are far more likely to receive responses and engagement. Meanwhile, when conversations are already dominated by familiar members talking with themselves, it can be very difficult for a new participant to break into the discussion and receive the same level of attention or interaction. Sure, some people will indeed try to welcome you. However, this is not guaranteed, and many newcomers may find themselves being ignored most of the time. This usually applies to almost every channel in the server, even including Japanese conversation channels.

    The community clearly expects individuals to shape and perform a recognizable personality in order to become noticed in the server. For newcomers, this expectation can be confusing and discouraging. Many people join with the intention of having casual conversations, asking questions, or simply interacting naturally with others. However, when attention and engagement tend to concentrate around already recognizable personalities, newcomers may struggle to find their place in the discussion. As a result, some may feel overlooked or uncertain about how they are expected to behave in order to participate meaningfully in the community.

🔨 The moderation team is actively disrespectful and discriminative. 🔧

This section applies to all members.
What do you think is the most important factor that makes a server engaging? Is it the members, the channel structure, or the rules? While all of these are certainly important, I believe that the moderation team is one of the most important factors in shaping how engaging a server truly feels. Moderators set the tone of the community. When moderation is fair, approachable, and supportive, people feel comfortable speaking, sharing ideas, and participating in conversations.

At the same time, members shouldn't feel constantly pressured or anxious about accidentally breaking a rule. When rules are enforced in an overly strict way, people may feel the need to constantly monitor everything they say. Instead of encouraging discussion, this can make conversations feel tense and unnatural. A welcoming community is one where moderation maintains order while still allowing members to relax, express themselves, and interact freely.

Phase 1A: Joining the Strong Ones

I initiated the first phase of this experiment by attempting to become part of the recognizable server members. {ex) being known by name, getting replies and engagement} First, I began by observing other recognizable server members and gradually adjusting my communication/texting style based on how others interacted. At first, the effects of the Matthew Effect prevented me from gaining attention immediately. (refer to sections above).
However, over time, as people began responding to me more consistently and recognizing my presence in conversations, I was able to confidently say that I had become a recognizable server member after about two months of engaging. Everything was going according to my expectations, and my hypothesis about the Matthew effect appeared to be correct.
